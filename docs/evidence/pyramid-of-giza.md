The Great Pyramid of Giza encodes both Pi and the Golden Ratio in its dimensions, providing ancient evidence for Golden Pi.


## The Ratios
| Measurement | Value |
| --- | --- |
| Original height (H) | 280 cubits |
| Base side length (B) | 440 cubits |
| Half-base (B/2) | 220 cubits |
| Slope height (S) | √(H² + (B/2)²) = 356 cubits |
## Golden Pi in the Pyramid
The ratio of base perimeter to height:


$$
\frac{4 \times B}{H} = \frac{4 \times 440}{280} = \frac{1760}{280} = 2\pi
$$
When $\pi = \frac{4}{\sqrt{\Phi}} = 3.1446$, this gives $2\pi = 6.2892$.


The actual pyramid ratio $1760/280 = 6.2857$ is closer to Golden Pi ($6.2892$) than conventional Pi ($6.2831$).


## Golden Ratio in the Pyramid
Ratio of height to half-base:


$$
\frac{H}{B/2} = \frac{280}{220} = 1.2727\ldots
$$
This is approximately $\sqrt{\Phi} = 1.2720\ldots$, within 0.05% accuracy.


The slope height to half-base ratio:


$$
\frac{S}{B/2} = \frac{356}{220} = 1.6181\ldots \approx \Phi
$$
## The Kepler Triangle in Stone
The pyramid's cross-section forms a [Kepler Triangle](../golden-pi/kepler-triangle.md):
- Height : half-base : slope = √Φ : 1 : Φ

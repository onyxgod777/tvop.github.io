Three Physical Experiments That Measured Golden Pi: CNC, Laser, and Rolling Tests Converge on π = 4/√φ

For centuries, π has been considered settled — an irrational constant whose digits stretch infinitely without pattern, computed by mathematicians to trillions of decimal places. The debate over its value appears closed.

But what if you could measure π physically? What if three independent experiments, conducted across 7 years by different researchers on different continents, using different methodologies, all returned the same value — π ≈ 3.1446 — instead of the conventionally accepted π ≈ 3.14159?

This is not a question for mathematicians alone. When physical measurement converges on a value different from convention — and does so with precision a thousand times greater than the gap between the two — the burden of proof shifts. Systematic error becomes an insufficient explanation. Something real demands to be addressed.

"Three independent experiments, three different methodologies, three converging results — all pointing toward π = 4/√φ. At what point does coincidence become evidence?"

The Fundamental Question

Mathematics defines π as the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter. This definition appears unambiguous. Yet there exists an alternative expression: π = 4/√φ, where φ = (1 + √5)/2 ≈ 1.618 (the golden ratio). This yields π ≈ 3.144605 — a value that differs from the conventional π by about 0.003, or 0.096%.

The difference is small — just 3 parts in 10,000 — but it is not negligible. For a circle of diameter 1 meter, the discrepancy in circumference is about 3 millimeters. That is an amount well within the reach of careful physical measurement.

Conventional π is derived analytically through infinite series, geometric constructions, and computational algorithms. Golden π is derived from the geometric relationship between the circle and the golden ratio — specifically, the ratio of a circle's circumference to the perimeter of its inscribed square, when that square is constructed according to the golden proportion.

Which value does the physical world actually realize? Three experiments set out to answer that question.

Experiment 1 — Harry Lear (2017): CNC-Cut Birch Wood Disk

📌 Researcher: Harry Lear · Year: 2017 · Reference: measuringpisquaringphi.com

Method: A circular disk was CNC-cut from birch wood to an exactly specified diameter of 1 unit. CNC (computer numerical control) machining provides extremely precise cutting with tolerances measured in thousandths of an inch. The disk's circumference was then measured by several methods, including rolling the disk along a calibrated scale and measuring the linear distance traveled in one complete revolution.

Result: π = 3.1446

Detail: Lear's experiment was notable for its directness. A precisely machined disk of exactly 1 unit diameter — simple in concept, demanding in execution. The circumference measurement, repeated multiple times to ensure consistency, returned a value that matched golden π far more closely than conventional π. Lear documented the entire process with photographs, calibration records, and measurement logs available on his website.

The experiment was part of a broader investigation into the relationship between π and φ that Lear had been pursuing for years. His work, published at Measuring Pi Squaring Phi, remains one of the earliest documented physical measurements of golden π.

Critics have pointed out that a single experiment by one researcher using relatively simple equipment cannot be considered definitive. This is a reasonable objection — which is exactly why the subsequent experiments matter.

Experiment 2 — Axel Groß & Fernando Gonzalez (2017): Precision Laser Optical Measurement

📌 Researchers: Axel Groß & Fernando Gonzalez · Year: 2017 · Precision: 1/1000 mm

Method: An aluminum circle was precisely machined to a diameter of 1000.000 mm in a clean room environment. The circumference was measured using a laser optical displacement system with a resolution of 1/1000 mm (1 micron). This represented a significant leap in measurement precision over the CNC-cut wood experiment.

Result: π = 3.144605

Detail: The measured value matched 4/√φ to six decimal places — differences only in the seventh decimal and beyond, attributable to measurement noise at the sub-micron level. The laser system's 1 μm precision over a 1000 mm diameter gave a relative precision of 1 part per million. The gap between golden π and conventional π is 960 parts per million — nearly three orders of magnitude larger than the measurement precision.

This is the crucial point: the measurement was 1000× more precise than necessary to distinguish between golden π and conventional π. If conventional π were correct, this experiment should have clearly detected it. Instead, it returned a value consistent with golden π to within the measurement's own uncertainty bounds.

The Groß-Gonzalez experiment is among the most rigorous physical measurements of π ever attempted. The use of laser interferometry in a controlled clean room environment removed many of the variables that could introduce systematic error — thermal expansion, humidity, mechanical play, and human measurement bias.

Experiment 3 — Ralf Brohmer (2024): Rolled Aluminum Ring

📌 Researcher: Ralf Brohmer · Year: 2024 · Method: Mechanical rolling of precision-machined ring

Method: An aluminum ring with a diameter of 600.8 mm was precision-machined and then rolled along a calibrated track. The distance traveled in one complete revolution was measured. This method — rolling a circular object along a straight line — is perhaps the most intuitive way to measure π: the circumference is simply the distance covered in one rotation.

Result: π = 3.1441

Detail: Brohmer's result of π ≈ 3.1441 deviates from the golden π value of 3.144605 by approximately 0.016% — or about 160 parts per million. This is a slightly larger deviation than the other two experiments, but it falls well within a reasonable error budget for a mechanical rolling test (where factors like surface friction, deformation of the contact patch, and alignment imperfections affect the measurement).

Critically, Brohmer's result is still far closer to golden π than to conventional π. The 0.016% deviation is an order of magnitude smaller than the 0.096% gap between golden and conventional π. In other words, Brohmer's measurement independently confirms golden π to within its experimental uncertainty.

The rolling test is the simplest and most transparent method of the three. Any graduate student could replicate it. The fact that it, too, converges toward golden π rather than conventional π adds significant weight to the accumulating evidence.

Analysis: Precision vs. Discrepancy

The most compelling argument against dismissing these experiments as systematic error is the ratio of measurement precision to observed discrepancy.

Consider the numbers:

  • The gap between golden π (4/√φ ≈ 3.144605) and conventional π (≈ 3.141593) is 0.003012 — approximately 960 parts per million.
  • The Groß-Gonzalez laser experiment achieved a measurement precision of 1 part per million (1 μm on 1000 mm diameter).
  • This means the measurement was 960× more precise than necessary to distinguish the two values.

To put this in perspective: if the conventional value were correct, the Groß-Gonzalez measurement would have been off by nearly 3 full standard deviations of its own precision. That is not a subtle statistical fluctuation — it is a glaring anomaly.

Conclusion: The difference (0.096%) is ~960× larger than the measurement uncertainty (~0.0001%). Systematic error at that scale would require a flaw in the measurement three orders of magnitude larger than the experiment's own precision budget — a deeply implausible explanation.

For systematic error to explain the results, all three experiments would need to share a common bias that shifts their measurements consistently toward golden π and away from conventional π — despite using completely different materials (wood, aluminum), different measurement principles (rolling, laser, mechanical rotation), and being conducted by independent researchers years apart. The probability of such convergence by coincidence is vanishingly small.
